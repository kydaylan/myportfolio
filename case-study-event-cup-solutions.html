The Brief

Event Cup Solutions (ECS) was created in 2020 as part of the eventhireGroup portfolio, providing reusable cups for festivals, stadia, and events. The company needed a website that could grow with the business — from initial launch through rapid scaling to eventual acquisition.

The Challenge

New Company, New Site

Brand new company in 2020 needed a website built from scratch to establish market presence.

Rapid Scaling

Business scaled rapidly, requiring the site to be rebuilt twice to match evolving commercial needs.

Sustainability Messaging

Sustainability messaging needed to be central to the brand identity, with reusable cups replacing single-use.

Dual Audience

Site needed to serve both B2B (festival organisers, venues) and B2C (direct purchases) customers.

Acquisition Transition

When the company was acquired in 2025, the website needed to transition smoothly to new ownership.

What I Built

Three iterations of the Event Cup Solutions website, each built on WordPress, with the site evolving from a simple product showcase to a full e-commerce and enquiry platform.

  • Version 1 (2020): Initial launch site establishing the brand and product range
  • Version 2: Rebuilt as the business grew and product offerings expanded
  • Version 3: Full remake to support the company's evolved market position and commercial requirements

But the website was only part of the story. I also designed the brand identity from scratch, created two product sub-brands, developed the cup messaging system, and produced all client onboarding artwork.

Brand Evolution

I designed the ECS brand identity as part of the eventhireGroup rebrand in 2022, replacing the original green logo with a cleaner identity that sat within the group's visual system. When the company was acquired by Re-uz UK in 2025, I transitioned the branding again.

Product Sub-Brands & Cup Design

I designed two product sub-brands for Event Cup Solutions. These aren't just logos on a cup — each cup is a communication tool that teaches customers a multi-step reuse process. The design challenge was creating messaging that stays consistent across every cup at every venue, while the actual return process varies depending on the location.

The Cup as a Communication System

Every cup had to guide the customer through a reuse process — typically 3–4 steps printed directly on the cup. The process changes by venue, but the messaging framework stays universal:

ONE Planet. ONE Chance.
Enjoy Your Drink / Return Your Cup / Don't Take Your Cup Home
ONE Smart Cup
I Am Reusable / Return Me For Auto Deposit Refund

Later iterations evolved the messaging. The ONE Planet cups simplified to: Enjoy Your Drink, Return Your Cup, Don't Take Your Cup Home.

The Smart Cup took a different approach entirely: "I AM REUSABLE — RETURN ME FOR AUTO DEPOSIT REFUND" — a single direct instruction designed for venues with automated deposit return systems.

This work went beyond logo design. I created two product sub-brands and a behavioural messaging system that had to work on a single physical surface — a cup — across hundreds of venues with different return processes. The constraint was designing something universal enough to scale nationwide while clear enough to change customer behaviour in the moment. Both brands are now printed on cups used at sports clubs, festivals, and events across the UK.

Client Onboarding & Marketing Artwork

As part of client onboarding, I created branded marketing artwork for a wide variety of sports clubs across the UK. Each piece was tailored to the individual club's branding to help promote reusable cup adoption at their venues. Deliverables included GIFs, short videos, social media posts, and static graphics.

Social Media Graphics: The Process in Action

I designed a series of Instagram graphics that walk customers through the reuse process step by step, using real event photography combined with the cup messaging system.

In the Real World

The system deployed at Gloucester Rugby (Kingsholm Stadium) and co-branded venue signage for Newmarket Racecourses.

What I Learned

Building the same site three times taught me that websites aren't finished products — they're living platforms that need to evolve with the business. Each rebuild wasn't a failure of the previous version; it was a response to genuine business growth that the earlier version couldn't have anticipated.

The cup design work taught me something different: designing for behaviour change on a physical product is a completely different discipline to digital design. You have one surface, no interactivity, and the user is holding a pint at a festival. The messaging has to be instant, universal, and impossible to misunderstand — while also working within different return systems at different venues.

The fact that I was retained post-acquisition to continue developing the site is validation that the work held up under scrutiny from the acquiring company. If the website had been poorly built, the SEO poorly managed, or the brand identity weak, that retention wouldn't have happened.
